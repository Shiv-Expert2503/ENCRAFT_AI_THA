MEP Drawing Interpretation 
AI Engineering Intern  ·  Suggested duration: 2 days 
This exercise reflects a problem we work on directly: the automated interpretation of MEP (mechanical, 
electrical, and plumbing) construction drawings for enterprise clients. The task below is a deliberately 
narrow version of it. We are evaluating how you scope, reason, and build, rather than the finish of the 
final product. 
The task 
Build a web application, runnable locally, in which a user uploads an MEP drawing and asks a question 
about it. The application answers using a vision-language model (VLM). 
Two requirements apply to every answer: 
– Grounding. The answer must show where on the drawing the information was found, with an 
indication of confidence. 
– Uncertainty. Where the system is not confident, it should flag the item for human review rather 
than guess.

The questions your application must answer 
Your application should answer all ten questions below, and the same application must work on both 
drawings without logic hard-coded to a single file. Every answer should indicate where on the drawing 
the information was found and flag anything uncertain. Where the correct answer is that an item is not 
present, the system should report that rather than return something that is not there. 
Drawing 1 — RXR Welcome Center HVAC plan 
1. How many ceiling supply diffusers are shown on this floor, and where is each one located? 
2. What return-air and transfer grilles are shown, and how are they distinguished from the supply 
diffusers? 
3. List the exhaust fans on the sheet and their approximate locations. 
4. What general notes apply to the diffusers and ductwork on this sheet — for example, anything 
specified for all diffusers or all duct runs? 
5. Does the sheet contain any flag notes or keynotes indicating an unresolved field condition or 
coordination conflict to verify? If so, summarise each.

1. Using this drawing’s legend, what is the difference between a supply air diffuser and a supply air 
swirl diffuser, and how many of each appear on the Upper Ground layout? 
2. What mechanical equipment is shown across the set (e.g. fan coil units, VRF condenser,

3. How many fire dampers are shown on the layouts, and where? 
4. Decode the abbreviations defined on the cover sheet (e.g. FW, TD, CP, DG, UC) and locate one

5. Distinguish refrigerant pipework from condensate pipework using the pipework legend, and 
describe where each runs. 
What to deliver 
– A runnable application — we should be able to start it locally from your README with a single 
documented command. 
– The source repository — your code, with the README described above. 
– A decision log (approximately two pages). Covering what you built and what you deliberately 
left out; the main technical choices and your reasons for them; where the system is unreliable and 
how you established that; and how you would extend the approach to large, multi-sheet drawing 
sets. Prose is sufficient; there is no need to build this part. 
– A 3–5 minute screen recording demonstrating two or three of these questions end to end. 
Guidance 
– We would rather see a smaller number of questions answered reliably than all ten attempted 
superficially. Prioritise accordingly, and record in your decision log what you chose not to do and 
why. 
– You may use AI coding tools such as Claude Code, Cursor, or Copilot, and we assume you will. 
We assess how you direct these tools and the reasoning behind your decisions, not whether you 
wrote every line by hand. 
– There is no single correct model or architecture. We are interested in your reasoning, including the 
alternatives you considered and set aside. 
What we assess 
We are assessing four things: your ability to work independently, to scope the problem sensibly, to 
deliver an application that runs, and to reason clearly about where a VLM-based system performs well 
and where it fails. A limited solution accompanied by an accurate account of its failure modes is 
stronger than an ambitious one presented as complete.
